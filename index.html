Mechanism

How the MEC Process Works

A Microbial Electrolysis Cell operates in three distinct stages — anodic oxidation, ion transfer, and cathodic reduction — to convert organic waste into pure hydrogen gas.

STEP 01
🧬
The Feed & Anode
Oxidation
  • Input: Organic waste (wastewater, acetate, food waste).
  • Process: Electro-active bacteria (EAB) act as biocatalysts in a strictly anaerobic (oxygen-free) reactor — they digest the organic waste.
  • Why anaerobic? Oxygen would quench the electrons before they reach the anode — the entire energy chain collapses.
  • Outputs — 3 things released: CO₂ — vented as gas H⁺ — protons swim in liquid e⁻ — electrons → metal anode
STEP 02
⚡
The Transfer
Membrane & Circuit
  • Liquid Phase: H⁺ ions travel through a Proton Exchange Membrane (PEM) to reach the cathode side.
  • Solid Phase: Electrons travel through an external wire to the cathode.
  • The Energy Boost: An external voltage of 0.2V–0.8V is applied to the wire.
  • Why the boost? Bacterial oxidation alone cannot push electrons over the thermodynamic energy barrier. The small electrical nudge is thermodynamically required — but the bacteria supply most of the energy from the waste itself.
STEP 03
🔴
The Cathode
Reduction
  • Process: Boosted electrons from the wire and swimming protons from the liquid reunite at the cathode.
  • The Reaction: 2H⁺ + 2e⁻ → H₂ ↑
  • Output: Pure Hydrogen gas is generated and collected.
  • Why H₂ specifically? The cathodic reduction of protons is thermodynamically favoured at low voltages — making MECs far more energy-efficient than conventional electrolysis.
ASPEN SUMMARY

To model this in Aspen Plus, we essentially need: [1] Anodic reactor (yielding CO₂, H⁺, e⁻) → [2] Membrane / separator (PEM ion transfer) → [3] External work/power input block (0.2–0.8V applied voltage) → [4] Cathodic reactor for the H₂ synthesis reaction (2H⁺ + 2e⁻ → H₂).

      Optimal Conditions & Thermodynamic Theory

      Best Configuration: Thermophilic Temp (55°C) • Thermoanaerobacter or Clostridium • Food Waste or Cheese Whey • Applied Voltage: 0.6V.

      Theoretical Basis: Bio-hydrogen synthesis is an endothermic reaction (ΔH° > 0). By the Van't Hoff equation, raising temperature increases the equilibrium constant K, shifting the system to favour H₂ production — but only up to the point where enzyme denaturation kicks in (~65°C). Similarly, voltage has an optimal window: too low (below 0.2V) fails to overcome the thermodynamic barrier, while too high (above 0.8V) drains grid electricity and destroys the energy efficiency ratio.
