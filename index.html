Bio-Electrolytic Hydrogen Synthesis

Exploring the thermodynamics of Microbial Electrolysis Cells (MECs) to generate sustainable Bio-Hythane via electro-active biological methods.

The Biological Engine

01

Anodic Oxidation

Electro-active bacteria (like Geobacter) act as living catalysts in an anaerobic environment. They consume organic waste, releasing CO₂, protons (H⁺) into the fluid, and transferring electrons directly to the metallic anode.

02

Thermodynamic Boost

While bacteria provide endogenous energy, a small external voltage (0.2V - 0.8V) is applied. This thermodynamic "push" allows the electrons to cross the energy barrier required for the final reduction phase, using a fraction of the energy of standard electrolysis.

03

Cathodic Reduction

Protons permeate the membrane while electrons travel the circuit. They reunite at the cathode (2H⁺ + 2e⁻ → H₂), synthesizing pure green hydrogen gas, forming the critical half of the Bio-Hythane energy mixture.
